A Novel Approach to Reduce Derivative Costs
in Variational Quantum Algorithms

This work presents a detailed numerical study of an alternative method, called Quantum Non-Demolition Measurement (QNDM), for efficiently estimating gradients and Hessians of quantum observables—a key, resource-intensive step in minimizing cost functions within quantum algorithms. We compare the resources required to achieve a fixed accuracy with those of state-of-the-art methods, showing that QNDM consistently requires fewer resources, even for small systems, with advantages expected to grow for larger, real-world implementations. A Python implementation of QNDM is provided in the supplementary material, making this approach a promising candidate for improving variational quantum algorithms on near-term quantum computers.

Resource Reduction for Variational Quantum
Algorithms by Non-Demolition Measurements

We compare two implementations of a variational quantum algorithm aimed at minimizing the energy of complex quantum systems: a standard projective measurement approach and a Non-Demolition Measurement approach. In the latter, gradient information is stored in a quantum detector before measurement. Using Lithium-based molecules as case studies, and later extending to more complex systems, we show that while both methods reach the energy minimum, the Non-Demolition approach offers significant resource savings. This advantage scales linearly with system complexity, making it an excellent candidate for efficient large-scale quantum optimization.

Quantum Gradient Evaluation through
Quantum Non-Demolition Measurements

This study introduces a Quantum Non-Demolition Measurement (QNDM) protocol to estimate the derivatives of cost functions on a quantum computer— a crucial step in implementing variational quantum circuits. By employing a quantum detector, QNDM enables direct derivative estimation, reducing the number of circuit iterations and logical gates required. The benefits are even greater when estimating higher-order derivatives. These results highlight QNDM as a practical and scalable alternative for running variational quantum algorithms on near-term hardware.
